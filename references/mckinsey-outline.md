# McKinsey-Style Outline

Use this structure for the master outline and every page outline.

## Principles

- Answer first: lead with the conclusion or recommendation.
- Pyramid structure: conclusion, reasons, evidence.
- Issue tree: split the topic into a small number of logical questions.
- MECE where practical: reduce overlap and gaps.
- One slide, one message: each page has one governing thought.
- So what: every page explains why the point matters to the audience.

## Master outline format

Include:

- `coreAnswer`: the answer-first conclusion of the whole deck;
- `audienceDecision`: what the audience should decide or do;
- `logicChain`: 3-5 major reasoning steps;
- `sections`: major sections with the question each section answers;
- `storyFlow`: why the sequence is persuasive.

## Page-by-page outline format

For each page, include:

- `pageNumber`;
- `title`: conclusion-style title, not a topic label;
- `keyQuestion`: the question this page answers;
- `governingThought`: the page's answer-first message;
- `supportingLogic`: 2-4 reasons, evidence points, or observations;
- `visualIdea`: the visual structure that best supports the logic;
- `soWhat`: why the audience should care;
- `transition`: why the next page follows.

## Visual logic mapping

- Compare options: side-by-side comparison.
- Explain drivers: driver tree, layered model, or waterfall.
- Show sequence: timeline, roadmap, or process.
- Prioritize: matrix, ranking, or funnel.
- Recommend action: chosen path plus reasons.
- Summarize system: architecture, operating model, or flywheel.

Do not use decorative visuals that fail to carry the logic.

## Expanded outline content

After the page-by-page outline is approved, expand each page before writing image prompts.

For each page, include:

- `expandedContent`: 1-3 short paragraphs that deepen the page logic with evidence, implications, and examples;
- `exampleIdeas`: 2-4 concrete examples, analogies, or mini-scenarios that make the point easier to understand;
- `visualEmphasis`: what the generated image should make most obvious;
- `speakerNoteHints`: brief reminders for final speaker-note writing after images are generated.

Do not write the final speaker notes at this stage. Final notes must be written after images are generated, using the approved outline and the final visual output.

## Final speaker notes

After the final images and HTML player are generated, write page-by-page speaker notes.

Speaker notes must:

- sound like a real presenter, not a written report;
- use clear, warm, direct language;
- explain the point with concrete examples when useful;
- refer naturally to what the audience can see on the generated page;
- avoid over-polished slogans, dense jargon, and reading every slide label.
